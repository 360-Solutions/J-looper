Building a looper app that works on a typical smartphone without an audio interface or wired headphones comes with a unique challenge: preventing the microphone from hearing and recording the app's own backing track (audio bleed).

The Latency & Bleed Compromise:
To solve this, J-looper uses a specific cycle. When you hit record, it waits one full cycle to sync up. Then, the backing track drops out while it records you. It’s like a spotlight breakdown moment where the band stops and you play alone, keeping perfect time on your own. Then, the rest of the band rejoins you at the beginning of the next cycle. To stay on beat, rely on your internal rhythm and the visual metronome—the white node traveling across the grid.

The "Cheat Code":
J-looper is a free JavaScript webpage, not a full-fledged App Store program. It works by briefly fooling your smartphone into thinking you're on a speakerphone call while you're in the recording window. That’s why you’ll notice a slightly "lo-fi" quality while recording. But when you hit "DONE" and return to the main screen, it releases the microphone and switches back to higher-quality audio.
